The Definition of Time

t = ⟨u|φ⟩ — a projection, not a parameter · Status: Derived

I

The Definition

In standard physics, time is a background parameter — a coordinate on which events are located. Newton treats it as absolute. Einstein treats it as relative to frame. Neither asks what it actually is. MPRC answers that question directly.

t = ⟨u|φ⟩
|φ⟩ Future — probability cloud, outer ring position, uncommitted. The ket without a bra is mathematically incomplete. That incompleteness is the future: real but unresolved.
⟨u| Present — the observer enters the frame, Least Action about to happen. The bra without a ket is also incomplete. The observer without a frame has no Least Action to perform.
⟨u|φ⟩ Past — the projection. A scalar. Finished. This is what the mathematics produces when bra meets ket: Newton's a→b and Einstein's relative-to-observer reading, unified.

The notation is not decorative. Each symbol earns its place. The structure of the bra-ket formalism already contained the physics of time before it was interpreted this way. MPRC reads what was already written.

II

Physical Grounding

The Extension of Einstein

Einstein showed that two observers at different reference frames measure different scalars for the same event. A lightning bolt is simultaneous for the platform observer and sequential for the train passenger. The event is identical. The scalar differs.

Einstein explained that frames produce different scalars. He did not explain why. MPRC provides that answer: Least Action is an in-frame operation. The scalar you read depends on whether you were inside the frame when it was generated.

The Braking Car — Physical Demonstration
A driver brakes from 80 km/h to 0. Two observers read the scalars 80 and 0. Both agree these events happened. For the Mars observer, 0 on the car's meter is still a large velocity relative to Mars — same event, different scalar, because the Martian cannot enter the frame the braking happened in.

One actor performed Least Action. Multiple observers read the collapsed scalar. Observation and Least Action are not the same thing. QM conflates them. MPRC separates them.

Observation — reading a collapsed scalar. Available to any frame-exterior entity.

Least Action — the act that produces the collapse. Only available to the in-frame observer.

The Propagation of Collapsed Scalars

A person commuting in Lahore uses fuel. A person sleeping in New York wakes to a higher fuel price. They never met. They share no frame. The collapsed scalar from one observer's Least Action propagated and became the probability cloud — the |φ⟩ — of an observer in a completely different frame.

This is the Shared Observer formally justified. Biology calls the same phenomenon herd immunity. Physics had no name for it because it had no frame theory that allowed collapsed scalars to propagate across non-intersecting frames. MPRC does.

III

Three Observer Hierarchy

Type 1 — Unitary Observer
Frame-exterior. Grants agency to in-frame bodies. Does not perform Least Action. Does not collapse. This is not a mystical claim — it is the logical complement of the in-frame observer. The Martian who watches the car brake is physically present at the event and structurally excluded from being the one who braked. Not by inability — by frame non-membership.
Type 2 — In-Frame Observer
Inside the ring. Accumulates φ. Performs Least Action. Generates the collapsed scalar. This is the driver who braked, the commuter who used the fuel. Frame-membership is what makes Least Action possible. Least Action is not a property of events — it is a property of who is inside the frame when the event happens.
Type 3 — Shared Observer
Never entered the generating frame. Receives the collapsed scalar as a boundary condition on their own |φ⟩. The person in New York who woke to a changed fuel price. The collapsed scalar from another frame's Least Action becomes their probability cloud — their future — without any frame intersection required.
